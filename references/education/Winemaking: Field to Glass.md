# Winemaking: Field to Glass
> A structured knowledge summary derived from the documentary transcript "A Perfect Vintage" (Troy Christian interview, TSC Gaming and Entertainment).

---

## Overview

Winemaking is the intentional transformation of grape juice into wine through fermentation. The winemaker's role is to choreograph the creation of the wine from vineyard selection through to the bottle — understanding the whole vision for a particular site and making it happen in both the vineyard and the winery.

---

## 1. Harvest Decision

**The most critical decision in the entire winemaking process.**

Factors considered when deciding when to pick:
- Sugar level (Brix)
- Acid level
- Impending weather
- Tank space availability in the winery

**Primary goal:** Keep fruit cool and intact; deliver it quickly to the winery.

Common practice: Night harvest (starting around midnight) into small quarter-ton lug boxes, weighed on arrival, then cold-stored before processing.

---

## 2. Sorting & Destemming

Sorting removes unwanted material — done by hand (4–6 people inspecting clusters on a table) or mechanically.

**Destemming decisions by variety:**
- **Pinot Noir:** Destemmed, then individual berry sorting. Some whole clusters retained for carbonic maceration effect (strawberry, grassy hay flavours).
- **Zinfandel:** Some whole clusters included for extended fermentation.
- **Syrah / Cabernet Sauvignon:** 100% destemmed.

**Whole berry / minimal crush:** Produces carbonic maceration effect; releases specific fruit-forward flavour compounds.

---

## 3. Red vs. White Winemaking — Fundamental Difference

| | Red Wine | White Wine |
|---|---|---|
| Skin contact | Fermented **with** skins and seeds | Juice separated from skins **before** fermentation |
| Colour source | All colour comes from skins | N/A |
| Tannin source | Skins and seeds | Minimised |
| Press timing | Ferment first, press later | Press first, ferment juice only |

**Rosé options:**
1. Red grapes pressed immediately and handled like white wine.
2. Saignée — juice drawn off a red must after short maceration.

---

## 4. Pressing

**White wine:** Bladder/pneumatic press. Gentle pressure. Juice separated from skins and seeds as quickly as possible to minimise phenolic extraction. Option to destem and leave on skins for a few hours to a day for more body and flavour.

**Red wine:** Basket press (single plunger). Pressed after fermentation. Length of skin contact before pressing depends on:
- Grape variety
- Fruit quality
- Intended wine style

> Mountain Cabernet: long skin contact for tannin and intensity. Lighter styles: pressed earlier.

---

## 5. Fermentation Vessels

Options: stainless steel, wood (open-top fermenters), concrete, French oak barrels (225 L barriques).

- **White wines:** Often fermented in barrel (barriques).
- **Red wines:** Stainless steel open-top or wood open-top fermenters.

Vessel size and material affect fermentation temperature. Modern tanks use cooling jackets for control.

---

## 6. Primary Fermentation

Sugar is converted to CO₂ and alcohol.

**Brix rule of thumb:** Alcohol % ≈ Brix × 0.6
> Example: 24° Brix → ~14.5% alcohol

**Yeast sources:**
- Cultured yeast (selected for specific attributes)
- Ambient/wild yeast from the winery environment

**Yeast requirements:** Sufficient nutrients and oxygen. Deficiencies cause stuck fermentations or reductive aromas.

**Pre-fermentation juice analysis:** Full juice panel (organic acids, nutrients, other parameters). Used to identify deficiencies for nutrient and acid additions.

**Temperature effects:**
- Red wines: High heat early → extracts colour and tannin.
- White wines: Cooler fermentation → preserves fruit and aromatic character.
- Some winemakers prefer lower temperatures for extended fermentation or fruitier style.

---

## 7. Cap Management (Red Wines)

During fermentation, CO₂ lifts skins to the top of the tank forming a "cap."

**Cap management frequency:** 1–3 times per day, adapted to vintage and variety.

**Methods:**
| Method | Description | Extraction level |
|---|---|---|
| **Pumpover** | Pump juice from bottom, wet the cap from above | Gentler |
| **Punchdown** | Submerge cap down into the juice | More extraction |

After fermentation: free-run wine drained off skins. Free-run is typically higher quality than pressed wine.

---

## 8. Malolactic Fermentation (MLF)

Conversion of malic acid → lactic acid via malolactic bacteria.

**Used for:**
- Chardonnay and other whites where reduced acidity is desired
- Imparting diacetyl (buttery) character
- Primarily a **stability tool**

**Prevented in:** Aromatic white wines (Riesling, Sauvignon Blanc, etc.) via low temperature, sulfur additions, or sterile filtration to preserve varietal flavours.

---

## 9. Élevage (Post-Fermentation Aging)

The period between fermentation and bottling.

**Duration:** A few months to several years depending on wine style.

**Barrel aging:**
- Historically practical (one person can handle a barrel; 50–70 gallon range)
- New oak: imparts flavour (toast level and wood origin matter)
- Old oak: no wood flavour but still beneficial — micro-oxygenation effect

**Bâtonnage (lees stirring):**
- Mixes settled yeast cells back into the wine
- Releases polysaccharides and mannoproteins → adds texture and body
- Particularly important for barrel-fermented Chardonnay

**Angel's share:** Evaporation from barrels. Topped up with fresh wine to prevent oxygen exposure.

**Racking:** Pumping wine from barrels into a tank, cleaning barrels, then returning wine. Done several times during élevage.

**Blending:** Can occur at any point. Typically after 8+ months to assess long-term flavour and character. Combines varieties, vineyard sites, or styles.

---

## 10. Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂) Management

**Purpose:** Antioxidant and antimicrobial preservative. Used in winemaking for hundreds of years.

**Application:** Added at multiple points throughout the process depending on fruit condition and wine style.

**Monitoring:** SO₂ levels reduce over time. Checked each time a barrel is opened; small additions made as needed.

---

## 11. Stability, Fining & Filtration

**Stability tools:** Chilling, fining, filtration.

**Filtration:**
- Removes sediment, yeast, bacteria
- Used when wine is hazy (loose filtration for clarity and taste)
- Critical when residual sugar, malic acid, or insufficient SO₂ could cause in-bottle refermentation

**Fining agents:** Egg whites, clay (bentonite). Improve tannin structure, clarity, or aroma.

---

## 12. Bottling

The last opportunity to influence wine quality.

**Timing:** When the wine has reached its apogee of balance and interest — not calendar-driven.

**Bottling lines:** Complex; many opportunities for error. Essential to quality despite being the least romantic step.

---

## 13. Wine Faults

**Most common source:** Microbiological spoilage.

**Prevention:** Clean cooperage, well-maintained barrels, wines kept topped.

**Common faults:**
- High volatile acidity (VA)
- Barnyard aromas (Brettanomyces)
- Cork taint (TCA) — always a flaw; has driven adoption of alternative closures

**Alternative closures:** Natural cork, agglomerated cork, plastic closures, glass closures (Vino-Lok), screwcap. Each differs in consistency, oxidation rate, flavour impact, cost, and consumer perception.

---

## 14. Closing Philosophy

> "Patience is very important. You can't be an impatient person when you're making wine. Decisions are very long and slow."

> "Every wine is a new experience. It's got its own journey and, if you listen carefully enough, it'll tell you what it wants to be and where it wants to go."

Fundamental principles of winemaking are universal, but practices vary by region, grape variety, and wine style — allowing winemakers to craft unique expressions.
