### Slide 5 — CSU: What Students Actually Learn

**Heading:** CSU graduates understand the science of every cellar decision — but are trained in principles, not real-time application

**Three deep-dive subjects:**

**WSC318 Wine Microbiology** covers: yeast species ecology during alcoholic fermentation; biochemistry of flavour and aroma production; bacterial ecology of malolactic fermentation; spoilage microorganism control; impact of chemical additions on microbial physiology. Students learn *why* SO₂ works, *why* MLF bacteria behave differently at different pH levels, and *how* to control spoilage — but not *what to do at 2am on Tank 7 with a stuck fermentation*.

**WSC319 Wine Chemistry** covers: fining agents (bentonite, PVPP, copper ion, blue-fining, polysaccharides); bitartrate stability; ion-exchange processes; enzyme treatments (pectolytic, glucanase, glycosidase). Students learn the chemistry of stability — but not the real-time calculation of bentonite rate for a specific protein-unstable lot.

**WSC317 Wine Science 2** covers: flavour compound diversity (monoterpenes, methoxypyrazines, esters); phenolic chemistry; anthocyanin structure and pH-dependency; polymeric pigment formation; influence of oxidation and wood treatment. Students learn the molecular basis of wine colour and flavour — but not how to adjust their current vintage's phenolic extraction in response to today's press data.

### Slide 6 — University of Adelaide: The Prestige On-Campus Route

**Heading:** Adelaide's Waite Campus winery gives students hands-on production experience unmatched in the Southern Hemisphere

**Body:**
The Bachelor of Viticulture and Oenology (BVitOenol) at the University of Adelaide is a four-year, full-time, on-campus degree taught at the Waite Campus — home to a working winery, vineyards, and world-class analytical laboratories. It is the only Australian program offering a dual viticulture and oenology qualification, producing graduates capable of working as either a viticulturalist or a winemaker.

**Year-by-year structure:**

| Year | Focus | Key Subjects |
|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | Science foundations | Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Foundations of Wine Science |
| Year 2 | Applied sciences | Microbiology, Sensory Studies, Introductory Winemaking, Viticultural Science |
| Year 3 | Advanced winemaking | Stabilisation & Clarification, Distillation/Fortified/Sparkling, Winemaking at Vintage, Engineering |
| Year 4 | Industry integration | Fermentation Technology, Waste Management, Industry Experience, Advances in Wine Science |

**Note:** No further intake from 2025 — program under review/transition. The Graduate Diploma in Viticulture and Oenology (GDVIO) continues as the postgraduate pathway.

### Slide 7 — Adelaide: Foundations of Wine Science I

**Heading:** Adelaide's Year 1 entry subject covers more ground in one semester than most industry workers learn in three years

**Body:**
The Foundations of Wine Science I subject (OENOLOGY 1018NW) is the entry point to the Adelaide program. It is deliberately broad, covering three integrated topic areas in a single subject: viticulture, oenology, and sensory evaluation.

**10 learning outcomes in a single subject:**
1. Describe the morphology, growth and development of the grapevine
2. Describe the seasonal cycle of growth of the grapevine
3. Explain the influence of light and temperature on photosynthesis, grape composition, yield and quality
4. Explain the physical and compositional changes in the grape berry during ripening
5. Outline the processes involved in winemaking before, during and after fermentation
6. Explain how winemaking principles from crushing to packaging influence wine style and quality
7. Differentiate the basic sensory processes and their application to technical wine evaluation
8. Recognise and evaluate basic sensory attributes of different wine styles using technical terminology
9. Communicate interpretation of experimental data in written form
10. Work effectively as part of a team (Small Group Discovery Experience)

This breadth in Year 1 sets the foundation for the deep specialisation that follows in Years 2–4.

### Slide 8 — UC Davis: The Global Standard for Wine Science Research

**Heading:** UC Davis defines the scientific standards that the entire global wine industry follows

**Body:**
The University of California, Davis, is the world's foremost wine science research institution. Its Department of Viticulture and Enology has produced foundational research in fermentation biochemistry, wine stability, sensory science, and viticultural management that underpins winemaking practice globally.

**VEN 002 — Introduction to Viticulture (2 units):**
Covers taxonomy, morphology, physiology, distribution, domestication, utilization, propagation, production systems, harvesting, and storage and processing of grapes. Entry point for the full degree program.

**Winemaking Certificate Program (professional development):**
Designed for production winemakers seeking to formalise their knowledge. Six learning outcomes:
1. Predict the impact of winemaking decisions on resulting wine quality and style
2. Use a systematic and scientific approach to assessing wine qualities
3. Evaluate wine stability and its impact on shelf life
4. Understand the principle mechanisms of sensory perception
5. Apply fundamental principles of analytical chemistry and microbiology
6. Assess the important factors in growing quality wine grapes

Entry requirement: one semester of university-level chemistry. The program is explicitly designed for practitioners, not students — making it the closest equivalent to Ownology's target user.

### Slide 10 — What Graduates Know vs. What They Still Need

**Heading:** The curriculum teaches the science — but cannot teach the 2am harvest decision

**Body:**
Across all three programs, graduates emerge with a deep, rigorous understanding of the science of winemaking. They understand fermentation biochemistry, microbial ecology, phenolic chemistry, sensory physiology, and the engineering of a winery. This is not a knowledge gap — it is a knowledge foundation.

**What formally trained winemakers know deeply:**
- The science of fermentation and the role of yeast and bacteria
- Wine chemistry: acids, phenolics, SO₂, fining agents, stability
- Sensory evaluation: fault identification, variety typicity, style assessment
- Winery engineering: equipment selection, utilities, waste compliance
- Regulatory frameworks for the grape and wine industry

**What no curriculum can provide:**
- The answer to *"my YAN is 95 ppm and Brix is 26.4 — what DAP rate for this Grenache, right now?"*
- Real-time, tank-specific, vintage-specific decision support
- Instant cross-reference of a winemaker's own data against their documented knowledge base
- A searchable record of every addition, measurement, and racking decision across a vintage

This is the gap Ownology fills — not replacing the science, but applying it in real time to the specific data in front of the winemaker.

### Slide 11 — The CSU Part-Time Pathway: Ownology's Primary User

**Heading:** The CSU student studying part-time during harvest is Ownology's most important user

**Body:**
A large and growing proportion of working winemakers are enrolled in the CSU Bachelor of Wine Science on a part-time basis — studying online while simultaneously running a vintage. They are learning the theory in one window and needing to apply it under pressure in another.

This cohort is simultaneously:
- Learning the biochemistry of malolactic fermentation in a CSU module
- Managing an active MLF in Tank 4 that is running slow
- Needing an immediate, evidence-based answer about whether to add nutrients, adjust temperature, or wait

Ownology is the natural complement to this learning pathway. It bridges the gap between the textbook and the cellar floor — providing the same winemaker who is studying WSC318 Wine Microbiology with an AI assistant that can apply those principles to their specific tank, in real time, during the vintage they are running while they study.

The CSU program has produced over 1,000 graduates in the industry. The pipeline of current part-time students represents Ownology's most engaged early adopter cohort.

### Slide 12 — Implications for Ownology

**Heading:** Ownology does not teach winemakers — it empowers what they already know

**Four strategic implications:**

**1. Assume competence, not ignorance.** Ownology's AI assistant should speak the language of a trained winemaker — using correct technical terminology, assuming familiarity with the science, and providing answers at a professional level. It is not a teaching tool.

**2. The value is in the intersection.** The science is known. The data is available. The gap is connecting them in real time. Ownology's core value proposition is the instant application of established principles to specific, current, winery data.

**3. CSU part-time students are the beachhead.** This cohort is actively studying the science while needing to apply it. They are the most motivated early adopters and the most likely to articulate Ownology's value to peers.

**4. The curriculum defines the knowledge baseline.** Every topic covered in WSC115–WSC321 represents a domain where Ownology's AI can provide expert-level, contextualised support. The curriculum is the map of what winemakers know — and therefore the map of what Ownology can help them apply.
