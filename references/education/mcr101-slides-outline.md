# MCR101 Learning Slides — HF Design Outline

## Human Factors (HF) Principles Applied

1. **Cognitive Load Management** — One concept per slide. No more than 5 key points. Use chunking to group related ideas.
2. **Progressive Disclosure** — Build from simple to complex. Foundations before mechanisms before applications.
3. **Dual Coding** — Pair text with visual diagrams/tables. Verbal + visual encoding improves retention.
4. **Spaced Repetition Cues** — Each slide ends with a "Key Takeaway" that reinforces the single most important concept.
5. **Signalling** — Bold key terms on first use. Use colour to signal importance hierarchy.
6. **Worked Examples** — Where possible, anchor abstract concepts to concrete examples (e.g., wine fermentation, food spoilage).
7. **Learning Outcome Alignment** — Each module maps explicitly to one of the 5 LOs.

## Slide Structure (12 slides)

### Slide 1 — Title / Course Map
- MCR101 Introduction to Microbiology
- CSU · School of Dentistry and Medical Sciences
- 14 Topics · 5 Learning Outcomes · 8 Credit Points
- Visual: subject map showing 14 topics grouped into 5 modules

### Slide 2 — Why Microbiology Matters (Topic 1)
- LO5: Interactions with human health, environment, agriculture, food/wine
- Hook: "Every glass of wine, every slice of cheese, every antibiotic — made possible by microorganisms"
- Key roles: Disease causation, food production, environmental cycling, industrial fermentation
- Applied angle: Wine/food/agriculture industries — direct relevance to CSU students
- Key Takeaway: Microbes are not enemies — they are the engine of life on Earth

### Slide 3 — The Microbial World: Who's Who (Topic 2 + 7 + 8 + 9)
- LO1: Describe structure and function of microorganisms
- The 5 major groups: Bacteria, Fungi, Viruses/Viroids/Prions, Parasites, Archaea
- Comparison table: prokaryote vs eukaryote; cellular vs acellular
- Size reference: bacteria 1–10 µm; viruses 20–300 nm; fungi up to 1 mm
- Key Takeaway: Classification by cell type and size determines how we study, culture, and control each group

### Slide 4 — Bacterial Anatomy: The Cell as a Machine (Topic 2)
- LO1: Describe structure and function of microorganisms
- Core structures: cell membrane, cell wall (peptidoglycan), cytoplasm, nucleoid
- Appendages: flagella (motility), pili (attachment), capsule (protection)
- Endospores: dormancy under stress — Bacillus, Clostridium
- Gram stain: Gram+ (thick peptidoglycan, purple) vs Gram− (thin + outer membrane, pink)
- Key Takeaway: The Gram stain is the single most important diagnostic tool in clinical microbiology

### Slide 5 — Seeing the Invisible: Microscopy & Staining (Topic 3)
- LO2: Processes used to study microorganisms
- Types: Light microscopy, phase contrast, fluorescence, electron microscopy (TEM/SEM)
- Staining: Simple stain, Gram stain, acid-fast stain (Ziehl-Neelsen for Mycobacterium)
- Resolution limits: light microscope ~200 nm; electron microscope ~0.1 nm
- Why it matters: Morphology + staining = first-line identification in clinical/food labs
- Key Takeaway: Microscopy is the primary language of microbiology — learn to read it

### Slide 6 — Growing Microbes: Culture and Cultivation (Topic 4)
- LO3: Cultivate and assay microorganisms
- Media types: nutrient broth (liquid), nutrient agar (solid), selective, differential, enrichment
- Isolation techniques: streak plate, pour plate, spread plate
- Anaerobic cultivation: anaerobic jars, glove boxes, reducing agents
- Nutritional requirements: carbon source, nitrogen, minerals, growth factors (vitamins)
- Environmental factors: temperature, pH, oxygen, water activity
- Key Takeaway: Every microorganism has a specific niche — culture conditions must mimic that niche

### Slide 7 — The Growth Curve: From Lag to Death (Topic 5)
- LO3 + LO4: Cultivate, assay, and control growth
- Four phases: Lag → Log (exponential) → Stationary → Death
- Measurement: turbidity (OD600), viable plate count, direct microscopic count
- Generation time: E. coli ~20 min; Mycobacterium tuberculosis ~24 hours
- Practical: in wine fermentation, yeast follows the same growth curve — YAN depletion occurs in log phase
- Key Takeaway: The log phase is when microbes are most metabolically active — and most vulnerable to control measures

### Slide 8 — Controlling Microbial Growth (Topic 5)
- LO4: Control growth of microorganisms
- Physical methods: moist heat (autoclave 121°C, 15 min), dry heat, pasteurisation, UV radiation
- Chemical methods: disinfectants (phenolics, alcohols, halogens, quaternary ammonium), antiseptics
- Antibiotics: mode of action — cell wall synthesis inhibition (penicillin), protein synthesis (tetracycline), DNA replication (fluoroquinolones)
- Testing: Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC), disc diffusion (Kirby-Bauer)
- Key Takeaway: Sterilisation kills everything; disinfection kills most — knowing the difference saves lives and product

### Slide 9 — Microbial Genetics: How Microbes Evolve (Topic 6)
- LO1 + LO2: Structure/function and study processes
- Genetic material: circular chromosome + plasmids; no nuclear membrane in prokaryotes
- Gene expression: transcription → translation; operons (lac operon model)
- Mutation: spontaneous vs induced; point mutations, frameshift mutations
- Gene transfer: transformation (naked DNA uptake), transduction (bacteriophage), conjugation (pilus-mediated)
- Significance: antibiotic resistance spreads via conjugation — horizontal gene transfer is the engine of resistance
- Key Takeaway: Bacteria evolve in real time — antibiotic resistance is evolution under selection pressure

### Slide 10 — Infection and Disease: Koch's Postulates (Topic 10 + 11)
- LO5: Interactions with human health
- Koch's Postulates (1884): 4 criteria to establish causation between microbe and disease
- Host defences: physical barriers (skin, mucous), innate immunity, adaptive immunity
- Normal flora: 38 trillion bacteria in the human body — mostly beneficial
- Pathogenicity factors: adhesins, toxins (exotoxins vs endotoxins), enzymes, capsules
- Virulence: degree of pathogenicity — LD50 as a measure
- Key Takeaway: Not all microbes cause disease — pathogenicity requires both the right microbe and a susceptible host

### Slide 11 — Immunity: The Body's Defence System (Topic 12)
- LO5: Interactions with human health
- Innate immunity: non-specific, immediate — phagocytes, NK cells, complement, inflammation
- Adaptive immunity: specific, memory-based — B cells (antibodies), T cells (cell-mediated)
- Antibodies (immunoglobulins): IgG, IgM, IgA, IgE, IgD — structure and function
- Antigens: any molecule that triggers an immune response
- Vaccination: active immunity via attenuated/killed pathogens or subunit antigens
- Key Takeaway: Immunological memory is why vaccines work — and why you only get chickenpox once

### Slide 12 — Applied Microbiology: From Cellar to Clinic (Topic 13 + 14)
- LO5: Agriculture, food, beverage, environmental applications
- Environmental: soil microbiology (nitrogen cycle, decomposition), water analysis (coliforms as indicators)
- Food/Wine: fermentation — Saccharomyces cerevisiae (alcoholic), Lactobacillus (lactic acid), Acetobacter (acetic acid)
- Industrial: antibiotics, enzymes, biofuels, bioremediation
- Agriculture: nitrogen-fixing bacteria (Rhizobium), biocontrol agents, biopesticides
- Wine-specific: MLF bacteria (Oenococcus oeni), spoilage organisms (Brettanomyces, Acetobacter)
- Key Takeaway: Microbiology is not just medicine — it underpins every fermented food, every antibiotic, and every healthy soil

## HF Design Notes for Slide Production

- **Background:** Deep navy #0D1B2A — reduces eye strain, high contrast for text
- **Accent:** Teal/cyan #00B4D8 — signals key terms and learning outcomes
- **Secondary accent:** Amber #F4A261 — for warnings, key takeaways, important distinctions
- **Typography:** Space Grotesk (headings) + Inter (body) — scientific, readable, modern
- **Layout variety:** Alternate between full-text, two-column, table, and diagram layouts
- **LO badge:** Each slide shows which Learning Outcome it addresses (top-right badge)
- **Key Takeaway box:** Bottom of each content slide — amber background, bold text
- **Progress indicator:** Slide number / total in top-right corner
