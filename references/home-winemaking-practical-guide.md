# Home Winemaking — Practical Guide (Field Knowledge)

*Sourced from: "Cooking with the Coias" — Ivo's traditional Italian home winemaking tutorial. This represents the tacit, hands-on knowledge of an experienced home winemaker making ~25 cases per year using no chemicals, no fining agents, and no commercial additives.*

---

## 1. Grape Selection

- Taste the grapes before buying — sweetness is the primary indicator of quality and alcohol potential.
- California grapes are reliably high in sugar as a general rule; higher sugar = higher alcohol.
- Refrigerated storage at the supplier is a good sign — it reduces mold risk before you even start.
- Common home winemaking varieties: Cabernet Sauvignon, Shiraz, Sangiovese, Ruby Cabernet, Zinfandel.
- You can also start from juice (not whole grapes) and pick up the process at the fermentation stage — same procedure applies.

---

## 2. Destemming and Crushing

- **Always remove stems.** Stems impart bitterness and can taint the wine. A machine crusher/destemmer handles both steps simultaneously.
- If doing it by hand: destem first, then crush using a manual crusher or (traditionally) by foot.
- The output is called **grape must** — crushed grapes, juice, and skins combined.
- For **white wine**: crush and press immediately (no skin contact needed — white juice inside even red-skinned grapes like Zinfandel).
- For **red wine**: crush and let must ferment on skins (see Section 4).
- For **rosé**: crush and press after a short skin contact (1–2 days).

---

## 3. Fermentation Vessel Setup

- Use a clean, sanitised open-top container (vat, drum, or food-grade plastic tub).
- Leave at least **30cm (1 foot) of headspace** — the cap will rise significantly during active fermentation.
- Cover loosely — fermentation gas must escape, but you want to keep fruit flies out. A clean cloth secured with a bungee cord works well.
- **Do not use a tight seal** during primary fermentation — pressure will build.
- Ideal location: insulated garage (not inside the house — fermentation gas is unpleasant indoors).
- **Temperature range**: 70–85°F (21–29°C) is ideal. 65°F (18°C) is acceptable but slows fermentation. Above 90°F (32°C) is too hot — cool the must down.

---

## 4. Sulfites (SO₂) — To Add or Not

- **Commercial wineries** add potassium metabisulfite to kill bacteria and prevent oxidation. Standard rate: **½ teaspoon per case of grapes ≈ 100 ppm**.
- Add sulfites **before fermentation begins**, if using them.
- **Home winemakers** can choose to make 100% natural wine with no sulfites. This is viable if you start clean and keep everything sanitised.
- Note: sulfites are a common cause of red wine headaches. Sulfite-free wine is gentler for sensitive drinkers.
- If you choose no sulfites, cleanliness and temperature control become even more important.

## 5. Yeast — Wild vs Commercial

| | Wild Yeast | Commercial Yeast |
|---|---|---|
| Source | Naturally present on grape skins and in the air | Purchased dried yeast packet |
| Control | None — results vary year to year | Consistent and predictable |
| Cost | Free | Small cost per batch |
| Risk | Unpredictable fermentation, possible stuck ferment | Low risk if rehydrated correctly |
| Flavour | Can add complexity and terroir character | Reliable house style |

**Rehydrating commercial yeast:**
- Use warm water (not hot — kills yeast).
- Rate: **0.073g of yeast per pound of grapes** (approximately 77g for 1,050 lbs / 25 cases).
- Dissolve in ~2 cups of warm water, mix well, then distribute evenly across the surface of the must.
- Commercial yeast will outcompete and suppress wild yeast once added.

**Measuring sugar (Brix / specific gravity):**
- Use a hydrometer. Drop it in a sample of juice, spin to remove bubbles, read the scale.
- 23 Brix ≈ 13.5% potential alcohol (a good target for full-bodied red).
- Track Brix daily during fermentation: 23 → 20 → 15 → 10 → 5 → 0 (dry wine).
- Racking is triggered when fermentation slows (Brix approaching 0, cap no longer rising strongly).

## 6. Cap Management (Punchdown)

- Once fermentation begins (typically day 2–3 if grapes were cold), the CO₂ pushes grape skins to the surface forming the **cap**.
- **Break the cap twice daily** — morning and evening — using a long-handled tool. Push the cap down and mix skins with juice.
- This extracts colour, tannins, and flavour from the skins.
- **Skin contact time and colour:**
  - 1–2 days: light red
  - 3–4 days: medium-dark red
  - 5–7 days: dark, full-bodied red
- **On the final day before racking: do NOT break the cap.** Let it sit so free-run juice settles below the cap — this makes racking much easier.
- Remove any stray stems you find during punchdown.

---

## 7. Racking (Free-Run Juice Removal)

- **Racking** = siphoning wine from one container to another, leaving sediment or skins behind.
- Rack when fermentation slows — the cap stops rising as high, CO₂ activity decreases.
- **Equipment**: a rigid tube (taped to a pole to keep it stable) with a small mesh screen at the bottom end, elevated ~2.5cm off the floor of the vessel. The screen prevents skin/seed blockage.
- Use gravity: position the source container higher than the destination.
- Fill the siphon tube with juice before starting to avoid air bubbles.
- Rack into clean **demijohns** (typically 52L / ~14 gallons). Fill to approximately 7/8 full — leave room for any residual fermentation.

**If the cap falls before you rack:**
- Not a disaster. Simply scoop everything into the press and press it all together. You lose the free-run separation but still get good juice.

---

## 8. Pressing the Skins

- After racking free-run juice, the remaining skins (pomace) still hold significant juice.
- Transfer skins to a **basket press** (or bladder press). Layer skins in the basket with the press cap on top.
- Apply pressure gradually — pause when flow slows, let juice drain, then press more.
- Use **ratchet straps** on old basket presses to prevent skins from escaping through the slats under pressure.
- Press until flow stops completely and the press is very hard to turn.
- The remaining **press cake** (dry pomace) can be composted. Some use it to make grappa (where legal).
- Press wine can be added to the free-run wine or kept separate (press wine is typically more tannic and extracted).

---

## 9. Secondary Fermentation Vessel (Demijohn / Carboy)

- Transfer all juice into clean, sanitised demijohns.
- Fit a **rubber bung with an airlock** (water lock). Fill the airlock with water or a dilute potassium metabisulfite solution.
- The airlock allows CO₂ to escape while preventing air from entering.
- **Air is the number one enemy of wine during aging.** Minimise headspace at all times.

**The marbles trick (headspace management):**
> If your final demijohn is only partially full after racking, add clean glass marbles to raise the liquid level to within 2–3cm of the bung. Marbles displace volume without adding anything to the wine. This is especially useful for the last, smallest container in a batch.

- Keep airlock water topped up — it can evaporate over weeks and months.
- In warm weather, wine volume expands. If the level rises toward the airlock, remove a small amount to prevent overflow and spoilage.

---

## 10. Racking Schedule and Timeline

| Event | Approximate timing |
|---|---|
| Crush and inoculate | September (harvest) |
| Primary fermentation complete | ~7–10 days post-inoculation |
| Transfer to demijohns | Day 6–10 (when fermentation slows) |
| First racking | 6–8 weeks later (November) — removes heavy lees |
| Second racking | ~4 months after first (March) — removes fine lees, clarifies |
| Bottling | August (11 months total) or later |

- The longer wine sits in bulk (demijohn), the clearer and more developed it becomes.
- If you have spare demijohns, let it sit for up to 2 years before bottling.
- After the second racking, top up to within 2–3cm of the bung and replace the airlock.

---

## 11. Bottling

**Equipment:**
- Rigid siphon tube with bottom cap (prevents sucking up sediment — sucks from above, not below).
- Manual bottle filler (stop-valve type): insert into bottle, valve opens when it touches the bottom, stops when lifted.
- Electric bottle filler: automated, stops at the correct fill level.

**Bottles:**
- Dark glass is preferred to protect wine from light degradation.
- If stored in a dark room, clear glass is acceptable.
- Reusing clean bottles is perfectly fine.

**Corks:**
- Natural solid cork: highest quality, more expensive.
- Conglomerate cork: ~half the price, acceptable for home use.
- Synthetic cork: more expensive than conglomerate, consistent seal.
- Use a corking tool (hand corker or floor corker) to insert corks cleanly.

**Sediment in the last container:**
- Do not discard the wine mixed with sediment at the bottom of the last demijohn.
- Pour it gently into a small container and use it to make **wine vinegar**: 1:1 ratio of this wine to white vinegar, let sit for 1 year. Nothing goes to waste.

## 12. Practical Tips and Tacit Knowledge

- **Wear clothes you don't mind staining.** Grape juice splashes and stains permanently.
- **Garage fermentation**: open the door regularly to vent fermentation gas. An insulated garage maintains temperature better than an uninsulated one.
- **Listening to fermentation**: active fermentation produces a crackling/fizzing sound. When it goes quiet, it is slowing down. Experienced winemakers use this as a racking cue.
- **The "boiling wine" (vena stambouli)**: traditional Italian term for active fermentation — the wine appears to boil from CO₂ activity.
- **Stems in the press**: the crusher/destemmer is not 100% efficient. Remove any stray stems you find during punchdown — they add bitterness.
- **Yeast consistency**: once you find a yeast you like, stick with it year after year. Consistency in yeast = consistency in house style.
- **Wild yeast variability**: wild yeast fermentations produce different results every year — this can be a feature (terroir expression) or a bug (unpredictability).
- **Aeration at bottling**: after a year in a sealed demijohn, the wine benefits from slight aeration when poured. This is normal and desirable at the drinking stage.

## 13. Troubleshooting

| Problem | Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| Fermentation hasn't started after 3 days | Grapes were cold; yeast not yet active | Wait — cold grapes take longer to warm up and begin fermenting |
| Cap fell before racking | Fermentation slowed too much | Press everything together; you still get good juice |
| Demijohn not full after racking | Volume lost to lees | Top up from another demijohn, or use glass marbles to fill headspace |
| Airlock level dropping | Evaporation over time | Top up with water or dilute metabisulfite solution |
| Wine level rising toward airlock | Temperature increase / seasonal expansion | Remove a small amount of wine to prevent overflow |
| Bitter taste | Stems not fully removed | Ensure thorough destemming; remove any stems found during punchdown |
| Headache after drinking | High sulfite content | Consider reducing or eliminating sulfite additions; ensure clean fermentation |
