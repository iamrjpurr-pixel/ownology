# Federal Regulatory Requirements for Australian Wineries

*Compiled May 2026 | Sources: Wine Australia Licensing and Compliance Guide, ATO, FSANZ, Safe Work Australia*

---

## Overview

Operating a winery in Australia involves compliance with a layered regulatory framework spanning at least five federal agencies. The Wine Australia Act 2013 and the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code form the two primary legislative pillars, but winemakers must also navigate the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) for excise and tax obligations, the Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry (DAFF) for biosecurity and export, and Safe Work Australia's model WHS legislation for workplace safety. State and territory laws add a further layer (covered in separate documents), but the federal framework applies universally regardless of location.

---

## 1. Wine Australia Registration and Licensing

### 1.1 Who Must Register

Any person or entity that produces, exports, or makes a wholesale sale of grape wine products in Australia must be registered with Wine Australia under the **Wine Australia Act 2013** and the **Wine Australia Regulations 2018**. Registration is required before any commercial activity commences. There is no minimum production volume threshold — even a single-case boutique producer selling at cellar door must be registered if any wholesale transaction occurs.

### 1.2 Annual Levy

Registered producers pay an annual **grape grower and winemaker levy**, administered by Wine Australia. The levy funds research and development, market development, and regulatory activities. The rate is set annually and is calculated on the volume of grapes crushed or purchased.

### 1.3 Label Integrity Program (LIP)

The **Label Integrity Program** is a mandatory record-keeping and audit system that underpins the legal validity of all claims made on Australian wine labels — vintage year, variety, and geographical indication (GI). The key rules are:

| Claim | Minimum Threshold |
|---|---|
| Vintage year | ≥ 85% of wine must be from that vintage |
| Variety (single) | ≥ 85% of wine must be from that variety |
| Variety (blend, e.g. "Cab Sauv Shiraz") | Listed in descending order; each ≥ 5% |
| Geographical Indication | ≥ 85% of wine must be sourced from that GI |

Winemakers must maintain **LIP records** for every batch of wine produced, including: grape receival records (variety, GI, vintage, weight), tank/barrel movement records, blending records, and bottling records. Records must be retained for **five years** and must be made available to Wine Australia on request. Failure to maintain LIP records can result in deregistration and prosecution.

### 1.4 Export Licensing

Any winery exporting wine must hold a separate **Wine Australia export licence**. Each export shipment requires a **Wine Australia export permit** and, for most markets, a **Certificate of Origin** or **Health Certificate**. Labels for export must be pre-registered in the **Wine Australia Label Directory** before shipment. Different markets impose additional requirements — the EU, UK, USA, and China each have specific documentation and compositional rules detailed in Wine Australia's Export Market Guides.

## 2. Food Standards Code — Standard 4.5.1 (Wine Production Requirements)

The **Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code**, administered by **Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ)**, governs the composition, additives, and processing aids permitted in wine production. Standard 4.5.1 is the specific standard for wine, sparkling wine, and fortified wine.

### 2.1 Permitted Additives

The following additives are permitted in wine production under Standard 4.5.1. "GMP" means the substance may be used at the lowest level necessary to achieve the technological purpose (Good Manufacturing Practice).

| Additive | Maximum Level |
|---|---|
| Sulphur dioxide (wine < 35 g/L sugars) | 250 mg/L |
| Sulphur dioxide (wine > 35 g/L sugars) | 300 mg/L |
| Sorbic acid / Potassium sorbate | 200 mg/L |
| Potassium polyaspartate | 100 mg/L |
| Tartaric acid | GMP |
| Malic acid | GMP |
| Lactic acid | GMP |
| Citric acid | GMP |
| Tannins | GMP |
| Gum Arabic | GMP |
| Metatartaric acid | GMP |
| Yeast mannoproteins | GMP |
| Carbon dioxide | GMP |

### 2.2 Permitted Processing Aids

Processing aids are substances used during production that do not perform a function in the final wine. Key permitted processing aids include: bentonite, gelatin, isinglass, egg white, casein (milk products), activated carbon, PVPP, diatomaceous earth, perlite, enzymes, oak, nitrogen, argon, oxygen, copper sulphate (GMP), dimethyl dicarbonate (200 mg/L maximum), potassium ferrocyanide (GMP, but residual iron must remain in the final wine), and cultures of microorganisms (yeasts and bacteria).

### 2.3 Water Additions

Water may only be added to wine where: (a) it is necessary to incorporate an additive or processing aid, (b) it is necessary to facilitate fermentation by diluting high-sugar must (must not be diluted below 13.5° Brix), or (c) it is incidental to the winemaking process. Total added water must not exceed 70 mL/L (7%) of the final wine volume.

### 2.4 Food Business Registration

Wineries are classified as **food businesses** under Standard 3.2.2 (Food Safety Practices and General Requirements) and Standard 3.2.3 (Food Premises and Equipment). This requires registration with the relevant **local council** as a food premises, maintenance of food safety practices, and compliance with premises and equipment standards (adequate water supply, drainage, pest control, cleaning and sanitising procedures, temperature control where relevant). Standard 3.2.2A (Food Safety Management Tools), which became mandatory in December 2023, may require some wineries to implement a food safety management system depending on their classification.

## 3. Wine Equalisation Tax (WET)

### 3.1 What is WET

The **Wine Equalisation Tax** is a federal tax of **29% of the wholesale value** of wine, administered by the ATO. WET applies to the last wholesale sale of wine in Australia and to wine imported into Australia. It applies to grape wine, grape wine products, fruit and vegetable wines, cider, perry, mead, and sake. WET is only payable if the entity is registered or required to be registered for GST.

### 3.2 WET Producer Rebate

Eligible wine producers can claim a **WET producer rebate** to offset WET liability. As of the 2026 federal budget announcement, the rebate cap is being increased from **$350,000 to $400,000** (effective 1 July 2026). To be eligible, the producer must:

- Be an Australian resident
- Have produced the wine themselves (or had it produced under their direction)
- Have made a wholesale sale, or used the wine for a taxable dealing
- Not be associated with another entity that would cause the combined rebate to exceed the cap

The rebate is claimed on the **Business Activity Statement (BAS)**. Producers selling exclusively at cellar door (retail, not wholesale) do not pay WET directly but must account for it in pricing.

### 3.3 GST Interaction

Wine is subject to GST (10%) in addition to WET. WET is included in the price on which GST is calculated. For retail sales, the effective combined tax burden is approximately 36.4% of the pre-tax wholesale value.

## 4. Labelling Requirements

### 4.1 Mandatory Label Elements

Under the Food Standards Code and the Wine Australia Regulations, the following information is **mandatory** on every wine label sold in Australia:

| Element | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Name of the food | e.g. "Wine", "Red Wine", "Shiraz" |
| Alcohol content | Expressed as % alc/vol, accurate to ±0.5% |
| Volume | Net volume in mL or L |
| Country of origin | "Product of Australia" or equivalent |
| Lot identification | Batch/lot code for traceability |
| Name and address | Producer or packer's name and Australian address |
| Allergen declaration | Mandatory if sulphites > 10 mg/kg, or if fining agents containing milk, egg, or fish products were used |
| Pregnancy warning | Mandatory pregnancy warning logo (introduced 2023, fully mandatory from 2024) |

### 4.2 Voluntary Claims and Restrictions

Certain voluntary claims are regulated. "Single vineyard", "estate bottled", "barrel fermented", and "organic" claims must be substantiated and not misleading under the Australian Consumer Law. The term "orange wine" cannot be used for skin-contact white wine unless the fruit is from the Orange GI. "Apera" and "Topaque" are registered certification trade marks of Wine Australia and may only be used by approved licensees.

### 4.3 Geographical Indications (GIs)

Australia has a registered system of GIs administered by Wine Australia. Using a GI on a label requires that ≥ 85% of the wine was sourced from that GI. The Register of Protected Geographical Indications and Other Terms lists all protected terms. Using a protected term incorrectly is a criminal offence under the Wine Australia Act 2013.

## 5. Biosecurity and Import Controls

The **Biosecurity Act 2015**, administered by DAFF, governs the movement of plant material into Australia. For wineries, the key obligations are:

- **Importing plant material** (vine cuttings, rootstocks, fresh grapes): requires import permits and compliance with biosecurity import conditions. Fresh grapes from most countries cannot be imported into Australia.
- **Phylloxera management**: wineries in phylloxera-free zones must comply with state-based phylloxera exclusion zone regulations (see state documents). Federal biosecurity requirements apply to interstate movement of vine material.
- **Winery waste**: grape marc, lees, and wastewater must be managed in accordance with biosecurity requirements to prevent the spread of vine diseases. Specific requirements vary by state.

## 6. Workplace Health and Safety (WHS)

### 6.1 Model WHS Framework

The **Work Health and Safety Act 2011** (model WHS Act, adopted in most states and territories) imposes a primary duty of care on persons conducting a business or undertaking (PCBUs) to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health and safety of workers and others. For wineries, the key hazards identified by Safe Work Australia and SafeWork NSW include:

| Hazard | Key Control Measures |
|---|---|
| **Confined spaces** (tanks, fermenters, pits) | Entry permit system, atmospheric testing for CO₂ and O₂, standby person, rescue equipment |
| **CO₂ asphyxiation** | Fixed or portable CO₂ detectors in cellar, ventilation, emergency procedures |
| **Hazardous chemicals** (SO₂, caustic CIP, acids) | SDS register, PPE, safe storage, spill containment |
| **Manual handling** (barrels, bins, hoses) | Mechanical aids, team lifts, ergonomic assessment |
| **Plant and machinery** (crushers, presses, pumps) | Guarding, lockout/tagout, operator training |
| **Working at heights** (tank tops, racking) | Guardrails, fall arrest, ladder safety |
| **Slips and falls** (wet cellar floors) | Non-slip surfaces, drainage, housekeeping |
| **Seasonal workers** | Induction, supervision, language/literacy considerations |

### 6.2 Specific Obligations

Wineries must maintain: a **WHS Management System** (or documented safe work procedures), a **hazardous chemicals register** and current Safety Data Sheets (SDS), a **confined space register** and entry permit system, a **plant and equipment register** with maintenance records, and records of worker induction and training. Incidents resulting in serious injury, illness, or dangerous occurrences must be notified to the relevant state WHS regulator immediately.

## 7. Environmental Obligations

While most environmental regulation is state-based, federal obligations include:

- **National Pollutant Inventory (NPI)**: wineries processing more than a threshold volume of grapes may be required to report emissions to the NPI.
- **Product Stewardship**: glass wine bottles are subject to container deposit schemes in most states (state-level, but with federal coordination).
- **Carbon reporting**: wineries making carbon neutral claims must comply with the Climate Active Carbon Neutral Standard administered by the Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water.
